HOUSE JOINT RESOLUTION 1328
By Moody
A RESOLUTION to honor and celebrate the 200th anniversary of
the Tabernacle Kinfolks Camp Meeting.
WHEREAS, in July 2026, the Taylor family will hold its 200th annual camp meeting in
Haywood County, commonly known as the "Tabernacle Kinfolks Camp Meeting," which is a
direct lineage from the traditions that began prior to 1773 at the tabernacle built by Colonel
Edmund Taylor in Mecklenburg County, Virginia; and
WHEREAS, Colonel Taylor was a third-generation Virginian, an Anglican, and a leader
in his community; he was on the Committees of Communication and Safety for Mecklenburg
County prior to and during the Revolutionary War, a job that was "not for the faint of heart."
Taylor's Ferry, across the Roanoke River, was a supply route serving the Southern Army
commanded by General Nathanael Greene; and
WHEREAS, the Taylors became "Methodists" before there was a Methodist
denomination, welcoming circuit riders into their homes as early as 1775, forming societies,
building gathering places, and embracing all the peculiarities of Methodism; famous circuit rider
and first Bishop of the Methodist Church, Francis Asbury, preached at the 1780 camp meeting
at the tabernacle on Colonel Taylor's property; and
WHEREAS, in 1784, the year the Methodist Episcopal Church was founded in America,
Colonel Taylor deeded the gathering place he had built near what is now Townsville, North
Carolina, to the Methodist Episcopal Church and named it Tabernacle M.E. Church; and
WHEREAS, by 1817, the Reverend Howell Lewis Taylor, a son of Colonel Taylor, had
filled his father's role in the community and enlarged that role in the Methodist Church. He was
ordained but never took up a circuit, preferring to be of service to those in his community; and

WHEREAS, in 1817, two of Reverend Howell Taylor's sons, John and Richard, decided
to move west for new opportunities. Reverend Howell, at the age of sixty-three, joined them on
their migration to the frontier of Tennessee; they crossed the Appalachian Mountains and
climbed the steep landscape onto the Cumberland Plateau; and
WHEREAS, in 1817, these Taylors settled as far west as they could go, to Montgomery
County, near the Red River, west of Clarksville. That same year, the Red River area became
the epicenter of the Second Great Awakening, where thousands attended the same type of
camp meeting events as the Taylors had begun in Virginia; the Taylors brought their tradition of
Methodist camp meetings to every community where they settled; and
WHEREAS, in 1818, the Taylors were joined in Montgomery County by John Williams
and family, neighbors from Virginia; and
WHEREAS, from 1805 to 1818, the Chickasaw Nation ceded portions of their hunting
lands in western Tennessee to the United States in exchange for property west of the
Mississippi River; in 1823, Haywood County was established from within the Chickasaw
Cessions; and
WHEREAS, in 1825, Richard Taylor bought 745 acres in Haywood County from John
Poston in Montgomery County; the Taylors were the third Anglo-Americans to have deed to the
property; and
WHEREAS, in 1826, the Taylors and Williamses brought their families and households,
including enslaved peoples, across the Cumberland and Tennessee rivers and down the vale to
Haywood County; they were the first to homestead northeast of Brownsville with some goods
sent by the river and the rest by wagon; and
WHEREAS, the very first day that the Taylors were on their Haywood County property,
they were joined by two circuit riders of the Methodist Church, Greenbury Garrett and Richard

Hutson, who rejoiced in seeing the others and were committed to building churches and starting
Methodist societies; and
WHEREAS, in the first year, the Taylors built cabins, barns, pens, cribs, and fences for
three homesteads and constructed a church on Richard Taylor's property. They named their
church New Hope; it was built of split logs, large enough so that all, Blacks and whites, could
attend services together; and
WHEREAS, a Methodist society, including like-hearted families—the Williamses,
Barcrofts, Curries, Jacocks, and others—immediately formed around the church with all the
peculiarities of that denomination: community gatherings for singing and shouting, confession
and conversion, preaching and praying, and in an immersive setting: camp meetings; and
WHEREAS, by 1832, all of Reverend Howell Taylor's children with their households had
joined their father and brothers in western Tennessee, and a larger church was built on Richard
Taylor's property. It was nicely hewn of logs and was, for the times, excelled by none, or at
least few, in the land; the name was changed to "Tabernacle Methodist Church," which was the
name of the home church in Virginia; and
WHEREAS, in 1847, an even larger church was built to accommodate the growing
congregation; with an addition and several remodels over the years, this church still welcomes
hundreds of kinfolks from all over the United States and the world for the annual Tabernacle
Kinfolks Camp Meeting, while continuing to serve the local congregation throughout the year;
and
WHEREAS, Reverend Howell Lewis Taylor, son of Colonel Edmund Taylor, and his
descendants, along with their neighbors and their descendants, have continued to stand for old
Methodism and to keep camp meetings from their years in Virginia, through their years in
Montgomery County, and through their 200 years in Haywood County; and

WHEREAS, we recognize that the importance the Taylor family's ancestors placed on
gathering community together in camp meetings for spiritual growth and deep fellowship
beautifully endures in their annual Tabernacle Kinfolks Camp Meeting; and
WHEREAS, we also recognize that Tabernacle Kinfolks Camp Meeting exists to
encourage and foster the faith journey of each participant, individually and collectively, and that
it is a community determined to continue its legacy of belonging and kinship that intentionally
seeks a deeper connection with God and with each other, even to the remotest generation; now,
therefore,
BE IT RESOLVED BY THE HOUSE OF REPRESENTATIVES OF THE ONE HUNDRED
FOURTEENTH GENERAL ASSEMBLY OF THE STATE OF TENNESSEE, THE SENATE
CONCURRING, that we honor the members of the Tabernacle Kinfolks Camp Meeting on
maintaining their family traditions and Methodist heritage for 200 years in Haywood County and
for 250 years of history in these United States of America, wishing them Godspeed as they
carry on this wonderful legacy and hope that they will continue to value and recreate this
treasured experience for another quarter-millennium.
BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED, that an appropriate copy of this resolution be prepared
for presentation with this final clause omitted from such copy and upon proper request made to
the appropriate clerk, the language appearing immediately following the State seal appear
without House or Senate designation.
