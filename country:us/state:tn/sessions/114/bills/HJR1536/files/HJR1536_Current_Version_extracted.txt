HOUSE JOINT RESOLUTION 1536
By Jones J
A RESOLUTION to honor the 150th anniversary of the Colored
National Convention held at the Tennessee
State Capitol in Nashville.
WHEREAS, the Colored National Convention held at the Tennessee State Capitol in
Nashville on April 5-7, 1876, was a significant Reconstruction-era assembly focused on voting
rights, civil rights, and political strategy for African Americans; its attendees included prominent
political leaders, educators, and organizers; and
WHEREAS, held during our nation's centennial year, the Colored National Convention
highlighted the disparity between American ideals of freedom and the lived experience of Black
citizens; and
WHEREAS, through the leadership of its prominent members, the convention passed
resolutions demanding Civil Rights Defense, a call for African Americans to use the courts to
defend their rights; Education Funding, a request for Congress to provide $7 million for public
education in the South; and Labor Reform, a demand for a Congressional committee to
investigate Southern labor and improve economic conditions, aiming for equal wages; and
WHEREAS, the 1876 Colored Convention was documented in contemporary media,
including a famous wood engraving, based on a photograph by Carl Caspar Giers, which was
published in Frank Leslie's Illustrated Newspaper on May 6, 1876; and
WHEREAS, this wood engraving vividly illustrates a historic moment in the struggle for
African-American civil rights by showcasing the gathering of Black leaders advocating for equal
rights, education, and economic opportunities during the Reconstruction era; and
WHEREAS, today, this piece serves as a powerful reminder of the resilience and
determination of those who fought for a better future; and

WHEREAS, the 1876 Colored Convention was part of a larger, long-term Black
convention movement spanning the 1830s–1880s that was aimed at asserting citizenship and
responding to the rise of violent oppression and disenfranchisement in the South through
political engagement and community unity; and
WHEREAS, overall, the Colored Conventions, such as the one held in Nashville in 1876,
reflect the long history of collective Black mobilization before, during, and long after the end of
the Civil War; and
WHEREAS, as empowering hubs of Black political thought and organizing, the Colored
Conventions provided space for informed public audiences to develop political plans and
community-building projects, celebrate racial unity and protest state violence, and work
tirelessly to secure Black people's civil rights; and
WHEREAS, as we celebrate the 250th anniversary of the founding of the United States
of America, it is most appropriate that we should honor those historic events that remind us of
how far we have come and how far we have yet to go; now, therefore,
BE IT RESOLVED BY THE HOUSE OF REPRESENTATIVES OF THE ONE HUNDRED
FOURTEENTH GENERAL ASSEMBLY OF THE STATE OF TENNESSEE, THE SENATE
CONCURRING, that we honor the 150th anniversary of the Colored National Convention held at
the Tennessee State Capitol in Nashville on April 5-7, 1876, reflecting upon its historical
significance and its pivotal role in assuring equality under the law for Black citizens.
BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED, that an appropriate copy of this resolution be prepared
for presentation with this final clause omitted from such copy and upon proper request made to
the appropriate clerk, the language appearing immediately following the State seal appear
without House or Senate designation.
