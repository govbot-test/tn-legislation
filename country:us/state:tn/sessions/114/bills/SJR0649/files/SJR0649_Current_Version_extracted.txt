SENATE JOINT RESOLUTION 649
By Johnson
A RESOLUTION to recognize and honor James Alvin Rogers and
his song, "Fly, Eagle, Fly."
WHEREAS, it is the privilege of the members of this General Assembly to recognize
those Tennesseans who are wholly devoted to their craft and enrich the lives of their fellow
citizens through their art; and
WHEREAS, one such individual is musician and singer-songwriter James Alvin Rogers,
whose song, "Fly, Eagle, Fly" was selected as an official State song of the bicentennial with the
adoption of Senate Joint Resolution 161 of the 89th General Assembly; and
WHEREAS, a resident of Franklin, Mr. Rogers was born in Chattanooga on December
22, 1949, to Hershel and Elizabeth Rogers; he grew up surrounded by a family that played
instruments and sang, and he received his first guitar at the age of eleven; and
WHEREAS, in 1958, he and his family moved across the State line to Fort Oglethorpe,
Georgia, where he began playing in groups; his senior year of high school was interrupted with
a six-month tour of active duty with the U.S. Army as a member of the Tennessee National
Guard, during which time he became famous for his "latrine concerts"; and
WHEREAS, James Rogers graduated from the University of Tennessee at Chattanooga
in 1974; though his initial intention was to become a lawyer, his desire to devote his life to music
proved a more potent force; and
WHEREAS, in 1975, Mr. Rogers signed with Capitol Records, and from that time until
1983, he traveled throughout the country, performing in large show rooms and in concerts with
some of the biggest names in show business, including Alabama, Suzy Bogguss, Roy Clark,
Steve Martin, Ricky Skaggs, and Dolly Parton; and

WHEREAS, widely recognized for his talent and musical contributions, Mr. Rogers was
the winner of the 1983 National Mountain Music Festival and has been inducted into the
Chattanooga Musicians Hall of Fame and the North American Country Music Association
International Hall of Fame; and
WHEREAS, for three decades, he was a star performer at Dollywood and at Music
Mansion Theater in Pigeon Forge, being honored in the categories of Best Entertainer, Best
Show, and Best Theater on several occasions by various outlets and organizations; and
WHEREAS, in addition to "Fly, Eagle, Fly," other compositions of his have been
recognized, as well, including "I Guard America," which was adopted by the Enlisted
Association of the National Guard of the United States as its official theme song in 1997; two
years later, the song was the official theme song of the National Guard at the Senior Leadership
Conference in Washington, D.C.; and
WHEREAS, much of his songwriting prowess has been channeled into raising
awareness of our country's living symbol of freedom, the bald eagle; "Where Eagles Fly" was
the theme song for the Fraternal Order of Eagles International, and "Save the Eagle" was
recorded by a slate of country music greats—Lee Greenwood, Ricky Skaggs, Tanya Tucker,
Deanna Carter, Terry Clark, Debbie Allen, Joy White, Ricky Lynn Gregg, the Oak Ridge Boys,
and James Rogers himself—for the American Eagle Foundation to help aid in the protection and
conservation of the bald eagle; and
WHEREAS, throughout his many accomplishments, Mr. Rogers has enjoyed the love
and support of his wife, Debbie, and their children, Heather and Justin; and
WHEREAS, it is fitting that we pause to honor the accomplished musician and
songwriter James Rogers, whose inspirational words and music have made a lasting impact on
the Volunteer State; now, therefore,
- 2 - 012369
BE IT RESOLVED BY THE SENATE OF THE ONE HUNDRED FOURTEENTH
GENERAL ASSEMBLY OF THE STATE OF TENNESSEE, THE HOUSE OF
REPRESENTATIVES CONCURRING, that we hereby honor James Alvin Rogers for his more
than half a century as a performing singer-songwriter and recognize his song, "Fly, Eagle, Fly,"
and its place in Tennessee history.
BE IT FURTHER RESOLVED, that an appropriate copy of this resolution be prepared
for presentation with this final clause omitted from such copy and upon proper request made to
the appropriate clerk, the language appearing immediately following the State seal appear
without House or Senate designation.
